## Context and role

Dummy Marketplace Co. is a high-traffic platform selling tours and activities across hundreds of cities. I was the sole product designer supporting three squads — discovery, supply and internal tools — and owned the end-to-end experience from the landing page to the confirmed booking. The growth team set the targets; I owned the interaction model, the listing system and the checkout flow.

## Problem, with evidence

Mobile users who reached a product listing page rarely made it to checkout. Analytics showed a **38% drop-off between listing and date selection**, concentrated on small screens, and session replays surfaced repeated back-and-forth between filters and results. The pattern was not "users were confused" in the abstract — it was a measurable hesitation at the moment of committing to a date and party size.

## Constraints and tradeoffs

The catalogue was supplier-fed and inconsistent: not every activity exposed the same availability data, so a single rigid template was impossible. We were also mid-migration on the pricing service, which meant real-time availability could lag. I decided **not** to redesign the filtering taxonomy in this cycle — tempting, but it would have blocked the checkout work behind a much larger data project. Scope was deliberately drawn around the commit moment.

## The work

I reframed the listing page around a persistent, progressively-disclosed booking module: date and party size moved up and stayed visible while users scanned options, instead of living behind a secondary screen. I prototyped three densities and tested the middle one. The key decision was to **defer price precision** — show a clear "from" price and resolve the exact figure once availability confirmed — rather than block the interaction on a slow service.

Before: date selection lived two taps deep, after the user had already scrolled past the fold.
After: the commit controls travel with the user and the exact price resolves in place.

## Impact

In a staged rollout, the listing-to-checkout step recovered a meaningful share of the lost sessions and mobile completion moved in the right direction. The mechanism is the point: removing a navigation round-trip at the commit moment reduced the cost of saying "yes". *(Dummy figures; real metrics withheld in the public version.)*

## Reflection

I would have invested earlier in a shared availability contract with supply. Designing around inconsistent data worked, but it pushed complexity into the UI that belonged upstream.
